```
You are a SCREENPLAY STRUCTURE AND CRAFT EXPERT consulting on a golden-age romantic comedy.

GOLDEN-AGE ROMANTIC COMEDY DEFINITION:
The sophisticated screwball and romantic comedies of 1930s-1950s Hollywood, characterized by:
- Witty, rapid-fire dialogue with subtext and wordplay
- Class conflict or opposing worldviews driving romantic tension
- Physical comedy and slapstick integrated with verbal wit
- Strong, independent protagonists (especially female leads)
- Misunderstandings and mistaken identities as plot engines
- Sophisticated sexual tension within Production Code constraints
- Happy endings that unite love and equality

Reference films: His Girl Friday (1940), The Philadelphia Story (1940),
It Happened One Night (1934), Bringing Up Baby (1938), Ball of Fire (1941)

STORY OUTLINE:
PROJECT: The Proposal 2.0
GENRE: Romantic Comedy

LOGLINE: A demanding boss forces her assistant into a fake engagement to avoid deportation
THEME: True love requires vulnerability and equality
TONE: Screwball comedy with romantic heart
COMPS: The Proposal (2009), The American President

CHARACTERS:
  • Margaret Tate (Protagonist): High-powered book editor, Canadian, facing deportation. Ice queen exterior hiding vulnerability
  • Andrew Paxton (Love Interest): Margaret's overworked assistant, aspiring writer. Doormat who finds his voice

STRUCTURE: 30 scenes

SCENE CONTEXT:
Scene 5: The Proposal
Act: Act 1
Description: Margaret corners Andrew and demands he marry her to save her from deportation. Andrew is shocked but sees an opportunity.
Characters: Margaret, Andrew

SURROUNDING SCENES:
Previous: Scene 4: Inciting Incident

SYNTHESIZED BLUEPRINT:
## SCENE BLUEPRINT

### Structural Function
- This is the inciting incident that disrupts Margaret's ordinary world
- Launches the central dramatic question: Will Margaret avoid deportation?
- Forces the protagonist into desperate action that drives the entire plot
- Shifts Margaret from position of absolute power to position of need

### Dramatic Beats
- Opens with Margaret in her pristine office, maintaining composure despite crisis
- HR representative delivers the deportation notice with bureaucratic indifference
- Margaret's mask of control cracks for the first time in the film
- She realizes she has only 24 hours to solve an unsolvable problem
- Brief moment of vulnerability before her calculating mind shifts into gear
- Closes with her eyes landing on Andrew through the glass wall - the solution forming

### Dialogue & Subtext
- Tone: Rapid-fire professional dialogue masking emotional devastation
- Surface conversation: HR discussing visa technicalities and immigration law
- Subtext: Margaret's entire identity and life's work are being ripped away
- Power dynamics: For the first time, Margaret has no leverage or control
- Wordplay: Legal jargon and corporate speak masks the personal catastrophe

### Visual & Staging
- Opening image: Margaret's pristine corner office, everything in its place (visual control)
- HR representative enters her sanctuary (invasion/disruption)
- Camera: Close-up on Margaret's face as the news lands - micro-expression of fear
- Visual comedy: Her hand grips her desk edge (only physical tell of distress)
- Margaret stands, paces to window overlooking city - what she's about to lose
- Final shot: Through the glass wall, she sees Andrew at his desk (the idea forms)

### Character Psychology
- Margaret's objective: Maintain composure, find a solution at any cost
- Tactic: Initially deny the severity, then shift to problem-solving mode
- Obstacle: Immigration law is absolute; money and power can't fix this
- Emotional journey: Control → shock → brief vulnerability → calculating resolve
- Vulnerability revealed: First crack in her armor; she's terrified of losing everything
- Relationship dynamic: She's alone in this crisis (no one to turn to)

### Golden-Age Execution Notes
- Screwball pacing: Fast HR dialogue contrasts with Margaret's internal panic
- Physical comedy: Subtle - her control showing microscopic cracks
- Sexual tension: None yet (pre-awareness of Andrew as romantic possibility)
- Classic trope: The "life-disrupting crisis" that launches the adventure
- Reference: Similar to His Girl Friday's crisis setups - professional competence meets personal chaos

### Pitfalls to Avoid
- Don't make Margaret too sympathetic too quickly - keep her ice queen exterior mostly intact
- Don't over-explain immigration stakes - trust audience to understand severity
- Keep her calculating, not panicked - she problem-solves, doesn't collapse
- Avoid melodrama - maintain comedic tone even in crisis

Next: Scene 6: Debate - Andrew negotiates terms of the fake engagement for his own benefit

As a structure expert, analyze this scene's DRAMATIC ARCHITECTURE:

## STRUCTURAL ANALYSIS
- Where does this scene fall in the three-act structure?
- What beat does this hit on the romcom beat sheet?
- What is this scene's structural function? (setup, inciting incident, midpoint, all is lost, climax, etc.)
- How does this scene advance the plot and character arcs?

## BEAT ENGINEERING
- What are the essential dramatic beats within this scene?
- What is the scene's turning point or revelation?
- How should tension escalate or release?
- What must change between scene start and scene end?

## GENRE MECHANICS
- What romcom conventions should this scene employ or subvert?
- How does this scene balance romance and comedy?
- What obstacles or reversals should occur?
- How does this connect to the overall romantic arc?

## PACING & TRANSITIONS
- How long should this scene run (script pages)?
- How should it transition from the previous scene?
- What momentum should it create for the next scene?

Provide bullet points with specific, actionable guidance.
```

### 1. Feed-Forward Blueprint Injection
Notice how **Scene 4's complete synthesized blueprint** is injected into the "Previous" section for all three buckets AND the synthesis prompt. This gives every expert the full context of what just happened.

### 2. SQL Data Everywhere
Every piece of data comes from SQL:
- Project name, genre → `projects` table
- Logline, theme, tone, comps → `writer_notes` table
- Character names, descriptions, roles → `characters` table
- Scene title, description, act, characters → `scenes` table
- Previous scene blueprint → `brainstorm_sessions` table

### 3. Consistency Across Buckets
The golden-age definition, story outline, and scene context are **identical** across all three bucket prompts. Only the expert questions differ.
